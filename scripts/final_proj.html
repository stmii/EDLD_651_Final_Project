College Retention Across Sectors

Author

Maritza Ruiz and Steven Todd McIntyre II

Abstract
While enrollment in higher education is decreasing, it is important to understand the relationships that different aspects of higher education institutions have with student retention. In this paper, we examine the relationships between institution type, starting intensity, and second fall retention rates in the United States from 2015-2023. We find that public and private 4-year institutions consistently have the highest retention rates, while private non-profit institutions consistently have the lowest retention rates. Furthermore, students starting at full time status are more likely in all sectors to be retained into the second fall. These findings can aide higher education policymakers in understanding both what can lead to student success and to evaluate part-time and non-credential status programs for possible improvements in retention.

Research Question(s)

What is the relationship between institutional type (public vs. private, 2-year vs. 4-year) and student retention?

How does enrollment intensity (full-time vs. part-time) interact with institutional type to influence persistence and retention?

Introduction

At a time when enrollment in higher education is declining, understanding how starting enrollment intensity (full-time vs. part-time) influences persistence and retention helps institutions identify barriers that affect student success and degree completion.

Colleges and universities serve increasingly diverse student populations, many of whom attend part-time due to financial, work, or family obligations. Examining differences across institutional types can reveal how structural and resource differences within higher education shape student outcomes.Research has consistently shown that first-generation and low-income students face higher risks of attrition, with dropout rates varying across institutional contexts (Cataldi, Bennett, and Chen 2018). For example, national data indicate that first-generation students are more likely to leave college early, particularly at public and two-year institutions where resources are more limited (Riehl 1994).

Recent statistics further highlight that nearly 40% of college dropouts are first-generation students, underscoring the importance of institutional type in shaping persistence (Bouchrika 2024).State-level data also demonstrate how retention rates differ across public institutions, with Oregon’s Higher Education Coordinating Commission reporting that 83% of students at four-year public institutions continued after their first year (Oregon Higher Education Coordinating Commission 2018).

These findings suggest that institutional type and enrollment intensity interact to influence student success, making it critical for higher education leaders to design targeted retention strategies, improve academic support systems, and create flexible pathways that promote persistence for all students, regardless of their enrollment status.

Methods

This analysis utilized R (R Core Team 2025) and the following R libraries: tidyverse (Wickham et al. 2019), rio (Chan et al. 2023), knitr (Xie 2014), here (Müller 2025), and janitor (Firke 2024).

Retention rates vary significantly by sector. Among individual sectors, “Public 4-year” institutions and “Private nonprofit 4-year” institutions demonstrate higher retention rates, suggesting these types of institutions may offer more consistent support structures or resources that promote student persistence. In contrast, “Private for-profit 4-year” institutions have the lowest retention rate, which may reflect challenges such as limited student services, financial instability, or differences in student demographics. “Public 2-year” and “Public PAB” sectors fall just above Private for-profit 4-year”, showing moderate retention outcomes. This distribution highlights the importance of sector-specific strategies when addressing student retention. Institutions with lower rates may benefit from targeted interventions, while those with higher rates could serve as models for effective retention practices.

Retention rates show differences in the various sectors over time. Again, private nonprofit and public 4-year institutions consistently maintain higher second-fall retention compared to other institutions, with private for-profit and public 2-year institutions showing the lowest overall retention rates. That said, for-profit institutions have seen an increase in retention from 2020 to 2023, even surpassing public 4-year institutions in 2023.

Across sectors, it looks like full-time students are substantially more likely to be retained into the second fall. Part-time status seems to have a relatively negligible effect on retention as compared to non-credential status, which may call into question the importance of degree-seeking status for non-full time students.
